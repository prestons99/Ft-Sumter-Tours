After the War

When the Civil War ended, Fort Sumter was in ruins. In the 1870s the US Army worked to restore it as a useful military installation. The outer walls of the gorge and right flank. largely demolished by shellfire, were partially rebuilt. The other walls, left jagged and torn 30-40 feet above the water, were leveled to approximately half their original height. Through a left flank casemate, where a barracks once stood, a new sally port was constructed. Eleven of the original first-tier gun rooms were restored with 100 pound Parrott rifles.

A shortage of funds slowed construction, and so from 1876 - 1897 Fort Sumter was used as a lighthouse station manned only by a "fort keeper" or ordnance sergeant. Even then, thousands of visitors came to the fort each year to see "where the Civil War began."

By the end of the 19th Century, the major world powers were building massive steel navies, and the US responded by modernizing its coastal defenses. In 1899 two 12-inch breech-loading rifled guns were installed at the fort. These guns were positioned on a massive concrete emplacement which was strengthened by earth fill extending to the top of the old fort walls. This battery, named for the South Carolinian Isaac Huger, a major general in the American Revolution, dominates the central portion of the fort today. The guns were removed for scrap in 1943.

During late World War II, Fort Sumter was armed with four 90-mm guns manned by a company of Coast Artillery. After the war, the fort was transferred from the War department to the National Park Service. It became a national monument on July 12, 1948.

In 1966 the site was listed on the National Register of Historic Places.
